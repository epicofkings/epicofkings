Shah • Na • meh  /ˌʃɑːhˈnɑːmeɪ/

Epic of Kings

Welcome to the Greatest Story Ever Told.

Legends. Heroes. Villains. Kings. Battles. Love. Heartbreak. Magic birds. Impossible choices.

The Shahnameh is the greatest epic of all time. But its real battle was not fought with swords.

When Arab armies conquered Persia in the 7th century, the Persian language was in danger of disappearing forever. For generations, it seemed like a battle that could not be won. Then a poet named Ferdowsi spent 30 years writing the Shahnameh. Thirty. Years. He wrote almost entirely in pure Persian, with barely a word of Arabic. Ferdowsi refused to let his people's voice die.

At more than 50,000 lines, the epic is nearly twice the length of the Iliad and Odyssey combined. Mythical characters, woven through every line, immortalize stories of real heroes and lost empires.

A thousand years later, the Shahnameh is still treasured throughout the Persian-speaking world.
Not only because it is a literary masterpiece, but because this single book kept a civilization alive.

Encyclopedia of Legends

Every major hero, king, villain, creature, and place in the Epic of Kings.

🌅
Kiumars
king
The very first king in the Shahnameh, Kiumars is the founding father of human civilization. He taught humanity to cook food, make clothes from animal skins, and live together in society. In the Shahnameh's telling, human history begins with Kiumars, making him the Persian equivalent of Adam. His reign was tragically cut short when Ahriman's son killed his beloved son Siamak.
"From one king, all civilization flows."
✦ Founding, Civilization, Primordial Kingship
⚔️
Siamak
king
The son of the first king Kiumars, Siamak was a brave prince who died fighting Ahriman's demon son in the world's very first great battle between good and evil. His death devastated Kiumars but also sparked the first war of vengeance in human history. Siamak's son Hooshang would avenge him.
"He was the first hero to fall so that others might stand."
✦ Courage, Sacrifice, The First Great Battle
🔥
Hooshang
king
The grandson of Kiumars who avenged his father Siamak's murder and became one of the greatest civilizing kings in the Shahnameh. Hooshang discovered fire by accidentally striking two stones together, taught humanity to work iron and steel, built irrigation systems, and established the festival of Sadeh. He is one of the great culture-heroes of Persian mythology.
"He struck stone on stone and lit the world."
✦ Discovery, Innovation, Cultural Progress
🐉
Tahmorris
king
The king known as "the Div-Binder" who captured demons and forced them to teach humanity the art of writing. Tahmorris subdued Ahriman himself and rode him across the world like a horse. He is credited with introducing thirty scripts to humanity. His reign represents the triumph of human civilization over supernatural evil through cunning and strength.
"He bound the demons and made them teach us how to write."
✦ Cunning, Civilization, Mastery Over Evil
👑
Jamshid
king
One of the greatest kings in the Shahnameh, Jamshid ruled for centuries and brought civilization to humanity. He invented iron, taught the crafts of weaving and medicine, and established the festival of Nowruz. But his pride brought him low — when he declared himself a god, the divine Farr (royal glory) abandoned him, and Zahhak rose to overthrow him.
"I am the light of the world. Through me, all things flourish."
✦ Wisdom, Pride (his downfall)
😈
Ahriman
villain
The great spirit of evil and darkness in Zoroastrian mythology, Ahriman is the cosmic opposite of Ahura Mazda, the supreme god of goodness. In the Shahnameh, Ahriman works through corruption and deception rather than direct confrontation. It was Ahriman who corrupted the young Zahhak, kissing his shoulders and causing the serpents to grow. Ahriman represents the eternal battle between light and darkness that runs through the entire epic.
"Evil does not always come armed with swords. Sometimes it comes with a kiss."
✦ Darkness, Corruption, Cosmic Evil
🐍
Zahhak
villain
The most fearsome tyrant in the Shahnameh. Zahhak was corrupted by the evil spirit Ahriman, who kissed his shoulders and caused two serpents to grow from them. The serpents demanded to be fed human brains every day. Zahhak ruled Persia for a thousand years of darkness until the blacksmith Kaveh rose against him and the hero Fereydun ended his reign.
"From his cursed shoulders grew the serpents of a thousand years of suffering."
✦ Cruelty, Evil, Corruption
⚒️
Kaveh
hero
A humble blacksmith who became one of the greatest symbols of justice in Persian history. When Zahhak's soldiers came to take his sons to feed the serpents, Kaveh refused and raised his leather apron as a banner of revolt. This apron, the Derafsh Kaviani, became the royal banner of Persia for centuries. Kaveh proves that the greatest heroes are not always kings.
"A blacksmith's apron became the banner of a nation."
✦ Justice, Courage, Defiance
⚖️
Fereydun
king
The hero-king who overthrew Zahhak with Kaveh's help. Fereydun bound Zahhak in chains beneath Mount Damavand and ruled Persia wisely for centuries. His greatest mistake was dividing his kingdom among his three sons, which led to tragedy, betrayal, and the murder of his most virtuous son Iraj.
"He who conquers a tyrant must also conquer the tyrant within himself."
✦ Justice, Wisdom, Fatherly Grief
⚔️
Manuchehr
king
One of the great early kings of the Shahnameh, Manuchehr was the king who avenged the murder of Iraj and restored justice to Persia. He was a strong and wise ruler who helped establish the heroic tradition that would eventually produce Rostam. The legendary Sam and Zal both served under his reign.
"Justice delayed is justice denied — but justice served is a dynasty restored."
✦ Justice, Leadership, Vengeance
🦅
Sam
king
The great champion Sam was one of the mightiest warriors of his age and the father of the white-haired Zal. When Zal was born with snow-white hair, Sam was convinced it was an evil omen and abandoned the infant on a mountaintop. He later repented and was reunited with his son. Sam served multiple kings faithfully and his family line produced Rostam, the greatest hero of the Shahnameh.
"Even the greatest of heroes can be wrong — and even the greatest of wrongs can be forgiven."
✦ Strength, Regret, Redemption
🌟
Simurgh
creature
The mythical bird of Persian legend, ancient beyond measure and wise beyond all creatures. The Simurgh lived on Mount Qaf at the edge of the world and possessed all the knowledge of the ages. She raised the infant Zal when his father abandoned him, and later helped both Zal and Rostam in their greatest trials. Her feather, when burned, would summon her instantly.
"She had seen three cycles of the world be born, flourish, and perish."
✦ Wisdom, Compassion, Ancient Knowledge
🏔️
Alborz Mountains
place
The great mountain range that runs across northern Persia, the Alborz is one of the most sacred and dramatic landscapes in the Shahnameh. It is where Zal was abandoned as an infant and raised by the Simurgh. Its highest peak, Mount Damavand, is where the tyrant Zahhak is chained for eternity. The Alborz stands as a boundary between the human world and the realm of myth.
"The mountains have kept their secrets since before the first king walked the earth."
✦ Sacred Landscape, Myth, Boundary Between Worlds
🦅
Zal
hero
Born with snow-white hair, Zal was abandoned by his father Sam on a mountaintop because of his unusual appearance. He was raised by the mythical Simurgh. When he eventually reunited with his father and fell in love with Rudabeh, he became one of the wisest counselors in Persia. Father of the great Rostam.
"The boy abandoned on the mountain became the wisest man in Persia."
✦ Wisdom, Resilience, Love
🌹
Rudabeh
hero
The beautiful princess who fell in love with the silver-haired Zal against all custom and convention. Rudabeh's courage in pursuing love despite her family's opposition is remarkable. When Zal climbed to her tower, she let down her own hair as a rope for him to climb. She later became the mother of Rostam.
"Love does not ask permission from kings or customs."
✦ Courage, Love, Devotion
🌺
Kabul
place
The ancient city of Kabul plays a pivotal role in the Shahnameh as the home of Rudabeh, the princess who became Rostam's mother. Her father Mehrab was a descendant of the tyrant Zahhak, making Rostam's birth a merging of the darkest and brightest bloodlines in Persian mythology. Kabul represents the complex, interconnected world of the Shahnameh, where enemies and heroes share the same roots.
"From Kabul came the mother of the greatest hero the world had ever seen."
✦ Royal Lineage, Complexity, The Birth of Greatness
🦁
Rostam
hero
The greatest hero of the Shahnameh and arguably the most powerful warrior in all of Persian mythology. Son of Zal and Rudabeh, Rostam's life spans hundreds of years and countless battles. He completed the legendary Seven Trials (Haft Khan), slew dragons and demons, and served as the shield of Persia through multiple dynasties. His horse Rakhsh was as legendary as Rostam himself.
"He was a man of iron, a hero who stood between Persia and the darkness."
✦ Courage, Strength, Loyalty
🐴
Rakhsh
hero
Rostam's legendary horse and faithful companion. Only Rostam was strong enough to ride Rakhsh — any other rider would have been crushed. Rakhsh was fiercely devoted to Rostam, even saving his life during the Seven Trials by killing a lion while Rostam slept. A hero in his own right.
"No other horse in Persia could carry the weight of Rostam's glory."
✦ Loyalty, Strength, Courage
🐲
Azhdaha
creature
The great dragon of Persian mythology, the Azhdaha appears throughout the Shahnameh as one of the ultimate tests of heroic courage. These massive serpentine creatures breathe fire and possess supernatural strength. Rostam slays a dragon during his Seven Trials, and dragons appear guarding ancient treasures and sacred places. The Azhdaha represents chaos, destruction, and the primal forces that heroes must overcome to prove their worth.
"Where the dragon sleeps, no ordinary man dares to tread."
✦ Ancient Evil, Chaos, The Ultimate Test
🐻‍❄️
The White Div
creature
The king of the demons of Mazandaran, the White Div was the most powerful creature Rostam faced in his Seven Trials. Rostam needed the White Div's blood to restore the sight of Kay Kavus and his blinded army. The battle between Rostam and the White Div is one of the most dramatic in the Shahnameh.
"Even among demons, there is a king — and Rostam killed him too."
✦ Power, Darkness, Defeat by Heroism
🌋
Mazandaran
place
The demon-haunted kingdom of Mazandaran is one of the most fearsome realms in the Shahnameh. When King Kay Kavus foolishly led his army there, the White Div blinded them all with black magic. It fell to Rostam to undertake his legendary Seven Trials to reach Mazandaran, defeat its demons, and restore sight to the blinded king and his army.
"Even the bravest army trembled at the borders of Mazandaran."
✦ Mystery, Danger, the Setting of the Haft Khan
🏛️
Zabulistan and Sistan
place
The homeland of Rostam and the legendary Sam dynasty, Zabulistan and Sistan form the heartland of Persian heroic tradition. This rugged southeastern region is where Zal was born with his white hair, where Sam trained his warriors, and where Rostam grew up to become the greatest champion in Persian history. The city of Zabul was their seat of power and the source of the mightiest heroes the Shahnameh has ever known.
"From these mountains came the strength that saved Persia a hundred times over."
✦ Heroic Homeland, Legacy, The Source of Champions
🔱
Kay Kavus
king
One of the more complex kings of the Shahnameh, Kay Kavus was brave but often reckless and vain. He attempted to fly to heaven using eagles, was captured by a demon king, and repeatedly placed Persia in danger through his foolishness. Yet he was also capable of great kingship, and Rostam faithfully served and rescued him time and again.
"A king with the heart of a lion and the wisdom of a sparrow."
✦ Bravery, Vanity, Dependency on Rostam
⚔️
Gordafarid
hero
One of the few female warriors in the Shahnameh, Gordafarid was a Persian noblewoman who donned armor and rode into battle against Sohrab when no male warrior would face him. She fought brilliantly, held her own against the mighty Sohrab, and used cunning to escape when she was finally unhorsed. Her name means "born of heroes" and she embodies the Shahnameh's recognition that heroism knows no gender.
"She rode out alone when all others stood back — and she rode back with her honor intact."
✦ Courage, Cunning, Defiance
🏹
Sohrab
hero
The tragic son of Rostam who grew up not knowing his father's identity. Sohrab became a great warrior and set out to find Rostam. Their eventual meeting in battle — neither knowing the other's identity — results in one of the most heartbreaking moments in world literature. Rostam kills his own son before learning the truth.
"He searched for his father and found him only in death."
✦ Courage, Tragic Fate, Love
🕊️
Tahmineh
hero
The princess who sought out Rostam in the night, drawn by his legendary reputation. She became his secret wife and the mother of Sohrab. Tahmineh raised Sohrab alone, telling him his father was dead to protect him. Her choice — made out of love and fear — set in motion the tragedy of Sohrab's death at Rostam's hands.
"She loved him enough to seek him out, and loved their son too much to let him go."
✦ Love, Courage, Tragic Consequence
🔥
Afrasiyab
villain
The great king of Turan and Persia's most enduring enemy. Afrasiyab was cunning, powerful, and ruthless, ordering the murder of the innocent Siyavash. His conflict with Persia spans generations of the Shahnameh and drives many of its central conflicts. Despite his evil acts, the Shahnameh portrays him with a certain tragic grandeur.
"For a thousand years, his shadow fell across the borders of Persia."
✦ Cunning, Power, Ruthlessness
🏹
Turan
place
The great kingdom to the north and east of Persia, Turan is Persia's eternal rival throughout the Shahnameh. Ruled for centuries by the cunning king Afrasiyab, Turan and Persia clash in wars that span generations. Many of the Shahnameh's greatest heroes cross the border into Turan and many of its greatest tragedies unfold there, including the murder of the virtuous prince Siyavash.
"Between Persia and Turan lay the battlefield where history was written in blood."
✦ Rivalry, War, The Great Enemy Kingdom
🌙
Siyavash
hero
A prince of extraordinary beauty and virtue, Siyavash is one of the most tragic figures in the Shahnameh. Falsely accused by his stepmother, he proved his innocence by riding through fire unharmed. He later chose exile and death over dishonor. His murder at the hands of Afrasiyab sparked one of the great wars of the epic. His name means "black horse" in Old Persian.
"He walked through fire and emerged pure. No accusation could stain him."
✦ Virtue, Honor, Tragic Innocence
⚡
Kay Khosrow
king
One of the greatest and most virtuous kings of the Shahnameh, Kay Khosrow was the son of the martyred Siyavash and grandson of Kay Kavus. He united Persia and Turan, avenged his father's murder by defeating Afrasiyab, and then made the extraordinary decision to abdicate at the height of his power rather than risk becoming corrupted by it. He simply walked away from his throne and disappeared into legend.
"He chose wisdom over glory — and in doing so, achieved both."
✦ Wisdom, Virtue, Legendary Restraint
🌺
Manijeh
hero
A princess who fell deeply in love with the Persian hero Bizhan and helped rescue him from imprisonment in a pit. When her father Afrasiyab discovered her love for a Persian, she was cast out and forced to beg for bread to feed the imprisoned Bizhan. Her devotion eventually helped bring about his rescue by Rostam.
"She gave up a throne for love, and love proved stronger than any throne."
✦ Devotion, Sacrifice, Courage
🗡️
Bizhan
hero
A young Persian hero who fell into the trap of Afrasiyab after entering Turan in secret to hunt wild boars. Imprisoned in a deep pit sealed with a great stone, he was only freed when Rostam came to his rescue after Manijeh revealed his location. His story is one of love, adventure, and the power of loyalty.
"In the deepest pit, he held onto hope — and hope did not abandon him."
✦ Courage, Hope, Loyalty
🌙
Lohrasp
king
The king who succeeded the legendary Kay Khosrow after his mysterious disappearance. Lohrasp was a just and capable ruler who established his dynasty and helped maintain the peace that Kay Khosrow had won. His son Goshtasp would become one of the most significant kings of the later Shahnameh.
"After the legend, there must come the law."
✦ Justice, Stability, Continuity
🔱
Goshtasp
king
King Goshtasp was the father of the mighty Esfandiyar and the king who embraced Zoroastrianism and spread its teachings across Persia. His reign marks a pivotal spiritual turning point in the Shahnameh. Yet he was also responsible for sending his own son Esfandiyar on a mission that led to tragedy, making him one of the epic's more morally complex royal figures.
"He found faith — but lost his son to it."
✦ Faith, Complexity, Tragic Fatherhood
🏔️
Esfandiyar
hero
A prince made nearly invincible through divine blessing, Esfandiyar was one of the mightiest warriors in the Shahnameh. Only his eyes could be pierced. His tragic conflict with Rostam is one of the most moving episodes in the epic — two great heroes forced into battle neither wanted, with devastating consequences. His story is a meditation on duty, fate, and the cost of ambition.
"He was steel forged by the gods themselves — and like all things forged too hard, he broke."
✦ Strength, Duty, Tragic Fate
🗡️
Shoghad
villain
The treacherous half-brother of Rostam and one of the most despised figures in the entire Shahnameh. Shoghad conspired with the king of Kabul to dig a pit of iron spikes to trap and kill Rostam. Even mortally wounded, Rostam had the strength to pin Shoghad to a walnut tree with his final arrow. Shoghad represents the ultimate betrayal: a brother's jealousy destroying the greatest hero Persia ever produced.
"Even the mightiest lion can be felled by the smallest treachery."
✦ Treachery, Jealousy, Fraternal Betrayal
🏛️
Persepolis
place
The magnificent ceremonial capital of the Persian Empire, built by Darius the Great around 515 BCE. Persepolis was a wonder of the ancient world — its grand staircases, towering columns, and intricate carvings depicted scenes of tribute from across the empire. Alexander the Great burned it in 330 BCE. Its ruins still stand in modern Iran, a testament to Persian civilization.
"Even in ruins, Persepolis speaks of a greatness that the world has not forgotten."
✦ Grandeur, Memory, Civilization
🌍
Mount Damavand
place
The great volcanic mountain that stands at the heart of Persian mythology. Mount Damavand is where Fereydun chained the tyrant Zahhak in a cave for eternity. It is the highest peak in Iran and the Middle East, and has been a symbol of Persian identity and resistance for thousands of years. Persians still point to it as the guardian of their homeland.
"Beneath its snow, the tyrant sleeps — and the mountain stands eternal watch."
✦ Eternity, Resistance, Sacred Power
🏟️
Rum (Rome)
place
In the world of the Shahnameh, Rum refers to the Byzantine Roman Empire to the west. Persian kings and heroes occasionally deal with the rulers of Rum in diplomacy and war. The inclusion of Rum in the Shahnameh reflects the ancient Persian awareness of the broader civilized world. Persia saw itself as the center of the world, with Rum to the west, Chin (China) to the east, and Turan to the north.
"Persia stood at the center of the world, and the world knew it."
✦ Geopolitics, Ancient World, Persian Worldview
🏮
Chin (China)
place
In the Shahnameh, Chin refers to China, the great empire at the eastern edge of the known world. Persian kings and heroes occasionally interact with the ruler of Chin, reflecting ancient Persia's awareness of the Silk Road civilizations beyond its borders. The Shahnameh portrays a world where Persia stands at the center, with Rum (Rome) to the west, Turan to the north, and Chin to the east, a remarkable vision of the ancient connected world.
"At the edge of the world, even China bowed to the fame of Persian heroes."
✦ Ancient World, Silk Road, Persian Worldview
🕊️
Homa Bird
creature
The Homa (also called Huma) is a sacred mythical bird in Persian tradition whose shadow falling upon a person confers royalty and great fortune. The bird never lands on earth but flies eternally through the heavens. Persian kings were sometimes said to rule because the Homa's shadow had blessed them. The Homa represents divine favor, destiny, and the sacred connection between heaven and earthly kingship.
"Blessed is the one upon whom the Homa's shadow falls."
✦ Divine Blessing, Fortune, Sacred Kingship
🐍
Zahhak's Serpents
creature
The two serpents that grew from Zahhak's shoulders after Ahriman kissed him. They could only be soothed by being fed human brains every day. The serpents became a symbol of tyranny consuming its own people. Even after Zahhak was overthrown, he was not killed — instead he was chained in a cave beneath Mount Damavand, where legend says he waits still.
"From each shoulder grew a snake, and each snake demanded the minds of the young."
✦ Evil, Corruption, Tyranny

Sources & Further Reading

Shahnameh: The Persian Book of Kings
Abolqasem Ferdowsi, translated by Dick Davis, Penguin Classics. The definitive English translation. Essential reading. View on Amazon →
Introduction to the Shahnameh, Shahrokh Meskoub
One of the most important scholarly works on the Shahnameh by Iran's foremost literary critic. Essential for deeper study of the epic's themes and cultural significance.
The Shahnama of Shah Tahmasp, Metropolitan Museum of Art
The most lavishly illustrated Shahnameh ever produced (ca. 1525-35), with 258 full-page paintings. A landmark of Persian manuscript art. View at the Met →
Folios from the Great Mongol Shahnama, Metropolitan Museum of Art
Scholarly essay on the Great Mongol Shahnama (ca. 1330-40), one of the most important illustrated manuscripts in Persian history, whose dispersed folios are held across major institutions worldwide. Read the essay →
Shahnameh Digital Collection, Cambridge University Library
A major digital archive of Shahnameh manuscripts held at Cambridge, with high-resolution scans of rare illustrated folios spanning centuries of Persian manuscript tradition. Explore the archive →
Stanford Iranian Studies Program
Stanford University's dedicated program for the study of Iranian history, culture, and literature. iranian-studies.stanford.edu →

Ancient Persia

Fascinating facts about the Persian Empire and the Shahnameh.

01
The First Declaration of Human Rights
When the Persian ruler Cyrus the Great conquered Babylon in 539 BCE, he freed all the slaves and let everyone worship their own god. He wrote his decree on a clay cylinder called the Cyrus Cylinder. The United Nations recognizes the Cyrus Cylinder as one of the earliest declarations of human rights, and a replica of the legendary cylinder sits in the UN headquarters in New York City to this day. Wow!
02
40% of the Entire World's Population
At its height, the Persian Empire was home to roughly 44 million people - about 40% of the entire world's population at that time. No empire before or since has ever ruled over such a large share of humanity.
03
One of the Oldest Living Languages in the World
At over 2,500 years old, Persian, also known as Farsi, is one of the oldest continuously spoken languages in the world. Unlike Latin, which evolved into French, Spanish, and Italian and is no longer spoken as a living language, Persian is still spoken today in almost the same form as it was in Ferdowsi's time. A Persian speaker today can read stories written 1,000 years ago!
04
The Persian Garden: Paradise on Earth
The English word "paradise" comes directly from the ancient Persian word "pardis," meaning a walled garden. Persian gardens were so breathtakingly beautiful, with flowing water, fruit trees, and geometric pathways, that they became the model for gardens across the ancient world, from the Hanging Gardens of Babylon to the gardens of the Mughal Empire in India.
05
Ancient Persia Gave Us Algebra
The Persian mathematician al-Khwarizmi (800-850 CE) invented algebra. The word "algebra" comes from the title of his book. He also gave us the word "algorithm." Every math class in the world owes him a thank you!
06
He Said the Earth Goes Around the Sun — 500 Years Early!
The Persian scholar Biruni suggested in the year 1000 CE that the Earth might rotate around the Sun — more than 500 years before Copernicus made the same claim in Europe! He also calculated the circumference of the Earth with remarkable accuracy using nothing but mathematics and careful observation.
07
The World's First Monotheistic Religion
Zoroastrianism, founded by the Persian prophet Zoroaster around 1500 BCE, is widely considered the world's first monotheistic religion, meaning belief in one supreme God. Its core teaching is simple and profound: choose good thoughts, good words, and good deeds. This ancient Persian faith directly influenced Judaism, Christianity, and Islam. The Shahnameh is steeped in Zoroastrian values — the eternal battle between light and darkness runs through every page.
08
The Tolerant Empire
Unlike most ancient empires, the Persian Empire let conquered peoples keep their languages, religions, and local leaders. Cyrus freed the Jews from Babylonian captivity and helped them rebuild their temple in Jerusalem. The Bible even calls Cyrus "the Lord's anointed."
09
History's Ultimate Warrior Challenge
In the Shahnameh, before Rostam could rescue the king of Persia from the demon kingdom of Mazandaran, he had to survive seven impossible trials alone: a lion, a waterless desert, a dragon, a sorceress, a demon, a rushing river, and a final battle. Each trial tested a different quality: strength, endurance, cunning, and wisdom. The Seven Trials (Haft Khan) are the Shahnameh's greatest adventure sequence, comparable to the Twelve Labors of Hercules in Greek mythology. Except Rostam completed all seven in one journey.
10
The Doctor Who Was 600 Years Ahead of His Time
A Persian doctor named Avicenna wrote a medical encyclopedia called The Canon of Medicine around 1025 CE. It became the standard medical textbook in Europe and the Middle East for over 600 years. He described how diseases spread through contaminated water and soil — centuries before anyone had heard of the word "germ"!
11
Persians Invented the Refrigerator
Over 2,400 years ago, ancient Persians built underground domed structures that kept ice and food cold even in desert heat of 40°C. The structure was called a "yakhchal". The word "yakh" means ice in Persian and the Persian word for refrigerator is still yakhchal today!
12
The World's Most-Read Poet Today
The best-selling poet in the United States today isn't American — he's a 13th-century Persian mystic named Rumi. His poems about love, God, and the human soul have been translated into dozens of languages and sell more copies in the US than any other poet. He lived 800 years ago and is still the world's most-read poet.
13
The Most Beautiful Ancient City Ever Built
Persepolis, the capital of ancient Persia, was so magnificent that when Alexander the Great conquered it in 330 BCE, his soldiers were reportedly stunned silent. The main audience hall had 72 columns, each 20 meters high. It took over 60 years to build.
14
The World's First Postal System
The Persians invented the "Chapar Khaneh" - a relay postal system with fresh horses stationed every 25 km across the empire. Herodotus wrote: "Neither snow nor rain nor heat nor darkness stops these couriers." Sound familiar? That's where the US Postal Service motto comes from!
15
Persia and Chess
The Persians are credited with spreading chess across the world. The words "checkmate" come from the Persian "Shah Mat" meaning "the king is dead." Every chess game echoes the Shahnameh — click here for our very own Epic of Kings version!
16
The World's First Windmill
Ancient Persians invented the world's first windmills around 500-900 CE, nearly 500 years before windmills appeared in Europe. These early windmills were used to grind grain and pump water, and their vertical-axis design was entirely Persian in origin. The wind that swept across the Persian plateau was put to work centuries before anyone else thought to try it!
17
The Royal Road: The Ancient Internet
The Persian Empire built a 2,700 kilometer Royal Road stretching from Sardis (in modern Turkey) all the way to Susa in Persia. Using fresh horses stationed along the route, royal messengers could cover the entire distance in just 7 days with a journey that would take an ordinary traveler 90 days on foot! It was the fastest communication network the ancient world had ever seen, thousands of years before the internet! Pretty cool!
18
Underground Water Engineers
Ancient Persians built "qanats" - underground aqueducts bringing water from distant mountains to desert cities. Some qanats were 70 km long and are still in use today, 3,000 years after they were built!
19
Hafez: The Poet Every Iranian Knows by Heart
The poet Hafez was so gifted that he memorized the entire Quran as a young boy — that's how he got his name, which means "memorizer." He became the greatest lyric poet in Persian history, and today nearly every Iranian home has a copy of his collected poems. Iranians still use his visionary verses as everyday proverbs, almost like a second language.
20
Nowruz: The World's Oldest New Year
The Persian New Year, called Nowruz, has been celebrated for over 3,000 years, making it the oldest New Year celebration still observed anywhere in the world today. It falls on the first day of spring and is celebrated by over 300 million people across Iran, Central Asia, and beyond. According to the Shahnameh, Nowruz was established by the legendary King Jamshid when he ascended to the heavens on a jeweled throne and the world filled with light.
21
The Epic That Saved a Language
The Shahnameh literally saved the Persian language. When Arab armies conquered Persia in 651 CE, Arabic began replacing Persian. Ferdowsi deliberately used pure Persian words with almost no Arabic. Because of him, Persian is still spoken by 110 million people today.
